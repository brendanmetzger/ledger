Attribution and Fair Use

An anecdote and oft-cited example, to be or not to be are short, insignificant words, but in that order, they are famous and remarkable.

It takes about two minutes of online searching to yield mountains of well-respected and readily available projects to view, take inspiration from, and borrow. In the briefiest version possible, name that inspiration (person or org) and mention the place (url) you found it and I we won’t schedule an uncomfortable conversation for when you don’t.

It will seem to you that program code is cryptic nonsense and cannot possibly be written by an actual human. Be warned, it is written by humans, and to humans that write a lot of code (ahem… me), its quite easy to identify an author vs. a cut & paster. Do not put yourself in a position where you are defending yourself against plagiarism—even if you meant no harm, at this level, integrity and authorship matters, and you will get in a heap of trouble for this. Regretably—and this seriously bums me out—turning in work you've copied is on the shortlist of extremely disrespectful things you can do to an instructor who is trying to help and guide you.

Style vs. ⌘V

One of the main areas of concern when evaluating student student work comes down to effort, and if you try, I'll be your champion. If I start to suspect that your work suffers on this front, I’m going to raise an eyebrow. Allow me to elaborate…

Without giving away all my tricks, let us gander at the adjacent CSS snippit.

  • The selector class names are first using a hyphen then an underscore in the second.
  • The .style-b's opening bracket is on its own line.
  • .style_a uses tabs, .style-b uses spaces (you'd be surprised)
  • The units have changed from px to em for no conceivable reason.
  • There is an extra space after the colon in the first style… typo? Who’s to say.

That’s 4 stylistic differences between two statements! I want everyone to understand the following from today forward: both examples are correct in terms of functionality. The glaring problem: inability to write syntax consistently indicates one is copying or guessing—and both are disappointing.
