CSS Units: length and size

Theory

Designing in terms of units may be something you have never thought about all that much, but understanding units is crucial to executing any feasible interactive project. Unlike an architect that knows the project site of a structure or a graphic designer that knows the dimensions of the paper they use, an interactive designer does not really know how their design may be realized. This can be difficult to wrap your head around but there are fundamentals that can be understood and applied right away.

Abandon pixels

Ask yourself, how wide is the viewport you are looking at? Is it universal—that is, am I just as likely to be using the same sized device as you right this moment? Of course not—viewport dimensions are variable.

Before any discussion of variables let us do a quick example of the foibles of trying to figure something simple out with a concrete number. Take a guess at how wide this viewport is—and to find out specifically. Resize your browser window and try again. I'll assume the best case scenario is that you could come up with a ballpark approximation on widths (or heights for that matter), but you are not getting the exact number and one of my personal tenets of interactive design is be exact. The only way to do that using px is to utilize some very clunky, outdated and hard to understand JavaScript wizardry.

Adopt Viewport Units

If abandoning pixels for measurement, how would one use a different unit to describe width. Perhaps the best method would be to use a variable unit. to measure the viewport width using vw units, and do the same resize song and dance. A variable unit doesn't always mean the same thing—which is precisely what a variable, in general, is. Our viewport is always, exactly, 100vw wide and we can work backwards from there. When designing, keep that property fresh in your mind—it is your landscape, your sheet of paper. Every composition from beginning to end relies on understanding dimensions. With variables, you don't need, and shouldn't use specific numbers anymore. If you happen to find yourself using a weirdly specific number, things are a mess somewhere.

Understanding percentages

Often misunderstood, sometimes confounding, working with percentages seems more intuitive than it is. There are some things to think about whenever you are using a % as a unit of measurement. The first is, percentage of what? The answer is the parent element determines the physical value of the percentage—not the viewport window. Here is a caveat: you cannot set a height so easily with a percentage. The height of a block depends on its content (think how text wraps), whereas the width actually determines how the content will wrap. Thus, a width can be determined, whereas a height can only be set if the height of the parent is known ahead of time. Of course, that can be done by actually setting the height non-percentage unit.
