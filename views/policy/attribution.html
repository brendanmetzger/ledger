Attribution and Fair Use

The internet is a vast trove of ideas. It has mountains of well-respected and readily available work to view, take inspiration from, and borrow. The two former items are just fine, but I wil focus on the later for the moment, and I want to be very clear; in an academic setting, borrowing without citation and proper attribution is definitionally plagiarism. I will give some examples in a minute, but before I do, remember this hard, unbending rule: If you didn't write it, cite it! That is to say, name the person who did, and mention the place (url probably) you found it.

Say you found some plugin you like and you copy and paste the code and add it to a project. It will seem to you—especially starting out—that program code is cryptic nonsense and easy to embed wherever. Be warned, to seasoned eyes it's just as easy to spot changes in technique that a beginner won't notice. Like a handwriting expert, a veteran programmer will see a signature has been forged. Also, there are plenty of programming tools to aid in searching and comparing code. Bottom line, do not put yourself in a position where you are defending yourself against plagiarism. Even if you mean no harm, mind the consequence; naiveté as an defense for academic misconduct is requesting a 0 instead of a meeting with an administrator.

Academic Integrity

I really don't expect to see any blatant plagiarism, but this is the writing on the wall. If I find proof that an assignment, midterm or final is a derivation of another's work, I will notify the proper academic authority. No matter what you think the assignment is, or how creatively you try to spin it, you should never think that finding something on the internet, putting your name on it, and changing a few variables will get me to look the other way. This really isn't hard to avoid, so, a few notes to help clarify.

Acceptable

  • All appropriated work has been noted; names, licenses and authors have been referenced in the code.
  • The plugin/framework was modified to make sense in your project – images, text, and other personalized content were added.
  • The plugin/framework and surrounding implementation does not represent the majority of the project.

Unacceptable

  • No attributions or citations given on something you didn't write; it is never implied
  • The plugin/framework was not significantly modified to be considered relevant to the project.
  • The plugin/framework and surrounding implementation represents a majority of the creativity/execution.

Danger Zone

One of the main areas of concern when evaluating student student work comes right down to effort, so if I start to suspect that your work suffers on that front, I'm going to raise an eyebrow.

Without giving away all my tricks, let's just look at something obvious to me, perhaps abstract to a novice. The brackets are now formatted on separate lines, the indentation has gone from tabs to spaces, the units have changed from pixels to ems, and there is an extra space after the colon in the first style. That's 4 stylistic differences between 2 CSS statements! Just imagine if there was an entire file like that.

This isn't necessarily anything bad here, you could just be super sloppy and I'll mark for that. But if it's more than sloppy, I'll figure it out.

Why I care about this.

In the big working world where everybody is busy (read: doesn't care), I know that we get away with poaching things that weren't sprouted via personal ingenuity. The real world is actually far worse—most of the time nobody even cares if the work is worth doing.

You can google just about anything, even in incoherent, malformed language and ole' google will do a pretty good job. Then there are better tools like stackoverflow where we can find solutions. I do it myself, but I've also been googling for over a decade, so let me outline some stuff I know to be true.

Google is an algorithm, not a human
Search engines, believe it or not, don't give measured advice, they return probability. I'll start with an anecdote: A while back I searched Tesla, figuring I'd get Nikola Tesla's Wikipedia page as the first result—the thing I was after.
The internet occasionally has mistruths
Resources have audiences
Books, tutorials, seminars, etc., can be intended for primary school students or post-secondary degree canditates.

Bottom line

  • You can (and should) scour the internet for resources
  • Cite anything that made it's way into your document via cut/paste.
  • Annotate any work you are unclear about
  • When in doubt, ask me ahead of time.
