The Web Development Workflow

Today’s lecture is titled mis en place, a French culinary phrase which means everything in its place, because it is the one thing every student can easily achieve that will guarantee a positive impact in any profession involving precision and craft. Having the proper resources and tools (and learning their correct usage) will both reduce the amount of mistakes and increase the amount of joy you feel with your efforts. I sincerely promise, learning a few tools efficiently will change the trajectory of your time in front of a computer forever.

Web Development as a Process

Iteration is not only beneficial for idea generation, but it also is paramount to successful project management. There are several things that impede good work—especially for those just starting. One of them is knowing where to start. Another is feeling like you grasp the material enough to actually author it yourself. The last, and biggest hurdle, is actually completing a project on time and satisfying clients.

Starting

Starting the idea for a project is difficult—especially when you have not had a bolt of inspiration. The way around this is to simply start and not look for something profound to happen right away. Designers should right away learn to not treat their work as precious.

Many professional designers know that a process of iterative improvement works well. You research topics, you sketch, you research, you refine the sketch, you research, you erase parts of the sketch and start over. Though the tool in this class is a keyboard and abstract symbols, we will honor the same iterative process; learn and research a little bit, get the fingers on a keyboard and apply an idea and stretch it out. Go to bed and do that again the next day for a little bit. Do not expect anything profound to happen—great design comes from lots of little steps that add up to something bigger.

Authoring

Authorship is not something to undervalue. Essentially it implies that you understand the techniques involved and the decision made to produce an outcome of your choosing. This is what separates professionals from amateurs.

Process plays the key role in authorship—and this course will encourage that iterative process. There is a technique {insert actual technique} in IxD that says the best way to prevent mistakes is to make them impossible. I will insure that plagiarism cannot exist by regulating the process by which you author material in this course.

The way this is done is through iterative development. That means that spending 5 straight hours in one sitting—which is not recommended anyway—is worth the less than spending an hour a few days of the week. The goal of stringing together several hours here and there is much more beneficial than the waterfall approach—both in developing concepts as well as refactoring design decisions.

Finishing

Finishing to some satisfying endpoint has many facets: personal, academic and of course, acknowledgement from your peers and advisors. In some ways, finishing is impossible—there is always something that can be tuned or adjusted, continued research leads to better and more interesting solutions. Knowing when to finish and move on is actually a skill that could be considered heuristic—that is, a personal solution may be best found through trial and error or a hands-on approach.

Finishing to a satisfactory end also means that you started in a very different place. A finished project is an order of magnitude different than the one you started with, and if it is not, then either you are the world’s best planner, or the project is likely pedestrian in concept and execution.

Techniques for success in this class

All of the techniques I am presenting come from professional experience on how to complete projects—especially on small teams where you have a great deal of personal responsibility. This is such a common phenomenon for developers and professionals to develop their skills that they coined the term life hacking to describe it.

Understand Parkinsons Law

Parkinson's Law is essentially an observation on the inability for deadlines to be met efficiently, especially when applied to larger organizations.

The Tomato (Pomodoro) Technique

The Pomodoro Technique is a way to plan for short, specific, focused spurts of work. Pomodoro means tomato in Italian (you can read the backstory on your own) and I have a version suited to this class.

  1. Decide on a research-based task to be done.
  2. Set a timer (traditionally to 25 minutes)
  3. Work on the task until the timer rings. If a distraction pops into your head, write it down, but immediately get back on task.
  4. After the timer rings, take a short break, grab a snack or beverage, stretch, walk about, etc. (3-5 min)
  5. Decide on a new code-based task (something from your research perhaps) to practice
  6. Set a timer (again, for 25 minutes)
  7. When the timer rings, you're done. Continue to jot down any distractions, or write down any goals for next time.

Now, this is perhaps the most crucial part. Your effort is time-boxed, that is, you can accomodate your schedule for little bits of work here and there. You will all of a sudden have an agenda and know what to work on next time you sit down. But, and this is important, the key for this technique to work is that you are going to employ it routinely. I recommend for times per week for success in this medium.

The Early Bird Technique

My personal tact—wake up early, focus on tasks you've thought about, end your computer day at noon. Spend the rest of the day exploring, reading, and noting what you'd like to do tomorrow.
