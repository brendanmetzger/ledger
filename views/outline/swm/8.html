Functions

The most important part of the way we write JavaScript is the notion of functions. Before we go down that road, we must understand a little bit about the namesake and the reasoning behind these methods. Functions are necessary, they are fascinating and they can even be beautiful.

In the beginning—and not particularly surprising considering the name—computers were primarily concerned with expediting rote computations. I've tried introducing functions in non-threatening ways, comparing them to cheeky things we know in real life; making pancakes and washing dishes—functions as routines. However, this doesn’t convey the the true import of functions, nor give much advice on how to use them correctly. Most often you’ll hear functions defined as a principle of organizing code to make programs reusable, decrease errors and avoid duplication.

In a more rigorous way of thinking, such a definition equates authoring functions as a means of satisfying a best practice, but that’s a bit misleading. Clarity and organization are certainly a byproduct of abstracting code, not the reason to do it. In their purest form, functions take input and return some sort of output. We already know about some functions already from the world of mathematics, so rather than invent a new function, perhaps it will be more beneficial to dissect an existing one.

We will spend the rest of class taking apart and thinking about a cosine, why it is a function, and what that means. We’ll make our own cosine function, all homemade, using a 300 year old technique. I expect this to be challenging, but you’ll walk away knowing a few things: there is no such thing as magic and small details can lead to big ideas.

While looking at the formula below, the first mystery we’ll see is that the cosine function (denoted cos(x)) is really a composition of two other mathematical functions and repeatable process (a loop). Make sure you are able to spot those three elements (hint, hint).
