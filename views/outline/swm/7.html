CSS Functions & the Last of the Pseudos

Our journey into the world of ‘pseudo’ stuff will also get us even closer to programming. We took a giant step forward with our first round of pseudos learning to apply styles depending on order and pattern through pseudo classes like :nth-child, and now introduce the limited ways that css can make a tiny bit of content on its own, the way you can utilize variables in a stylesheet and learn methods of the calc attribute to do some interesting tricks.

I say the new round of pseudo stuff takes us closer to programming because now we are really looking at design and development through a lens of calculations and conditions. Designing this way is abstract and once learned, signifiantly more powerful than one-dimensional stuff.

:not()
A powerful technique I find invaluable, this pseudo selector negates any selector inside the argument.
ex: p:not(.special) {...}selects all paragraphs that do not have a class of special.
ex: section p:not(:first-of-type) {...}selects all paragraphs of a section except the first one.
The attr function
This is a pretty nifty function.
:before & :after
These pseudo-elements are a nice trick to add custom bullets, icons, or bits of boiler plate text to certain elements.
p:after {content: '...'} would add ‘...’ after of every paragraph.
abbr:before {content: attr(title)} would write the title attribute before each abbreviation.
:first-letter and :first-line
Another pair of pseudo elements that are somewhat self explanatory.
p:first-letter {font-size:500%;} would make the first letter of every paragraph 500% larger than the adjacent text.
p:first-line {font-weight: 800} would make the first line of text in every paragraph bold.
