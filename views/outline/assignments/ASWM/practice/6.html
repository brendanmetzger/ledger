Exchange

Last week you wrote a little program, uncommented, employing formatting techniques. This week, you'll simply take two other programs, written by a course-mate, and refactor that code.

The takeaway here is I want you to learn what reading someone else’s code is like. Can you both read someone else’s code and improve it in some way? Likewise, will someone be able to read and understand your code? Mind, improve is very subjective—it could mean making it more legible, clarifying obscure points through rewriting code, optimizing inefficient algorithms, or if you want, adding a feature or two.

The rest of the semester

I've also put some thoughts into where we should be spending our energy as many in this room are finishing up their college career. So I propose this the following. Graduating folk, I think the rest of the semester you should engage yourself with a big project, and work towards it every week. Non-graduating folk, I think you should pick a front-end discipline with a lot of crossover (like SVG), to occupy your efforts and try to build up a strong understanding of concepts that will help you greatly in the coming semesters before matriculation. Together, we will use the majority of class time as a forum to work through problems together.

Concepts to focus on

As web developers and program designers, there is plenty of craft to be honed, and practice to do. While I want you very much to work towards a big project, I also want you to practice techniques every week, and use your practice area to explore smaller ideas that can be picked apart and shared with everyone. Here is some stuff that I thought we would cover, but I'm going to leave it up to you to see if it fits in with work you are trying to do:

  • Function Binding
  • this and object prototypes
  • Topics in functional programming (map/reduce/filter)
  • Frameworks and HTML5 APIs
